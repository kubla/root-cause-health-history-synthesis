# Branch Guides

Open branches selectively. Each branch should answer one question:

What information from this domain would materially improve the timeline, causal map, or handoff artifact?

## GI, bowel, food response

Ask when:

- GI symptoms are dominant
- food response seems causally relevant
- fatigue, skin, mood, or systemic symptoms may be linked to digestion

Capture:

- onset and timing relative to the main illness story
- bowel pattern and changes
- pain, bloating, reflux, nausea, urgency, constipation, diarrhea
- meal timing and food-response patterns
- elimination diets, supplements, enzymes, probiotics, medications
- infections, antibiotics, travel, food poisoning
- weight change and appetite change

What moves the causal map:

- repeatable food-response patterns
- post-infectious shifts
- antibiotic or medication associations
- relief patterns that imply mechanisms

## Sleep and circadian

Ask when:

- fatigue, cognition, mood, headaches, blood pressure, or pain may depend on sleep

Capture:

- baseline sleep before illness
- sleep onset, maintenance, timing, and variability
- nonrestorative sleep
- naps and daytime sleepiness
- snoring, witnessed apneas, gasping
- shift work, jet lag, light timing, bedtime drift
- response to sleep interventions

What moves the causal map:

- abrupt change in sleep around onset
- circadian instability
- signs of sleep-disordered breathing
- mismatch between time asleep and restoration

## Mood, stress, and cognition

Ask when:

- attention, memory, anxiety, mood, stress load, or burnout may mediate symptoms

Capture:

- mood and cognition before illness
- changes in concentration, memory, motivation, irritability, anxiety
- life stressors and periods of overload
- coping patterns and supports
- panic, depersonalization, trauma triggers if relevant
- psychiatric history, therapy, medications, response history

What moves the causal map:

- timing of symptom flares with stress or burnout
- cognitive changes that track with sleep, inflammation, or exertion
- treatment responses that separate primary from secondary mood symptoms

## Hormonal and reproductive

Ask when:

- symptoms cluster around menstrual cycles, fertility changes, puberty, pregnancy, postpartum periods, or androgen or thyroid concerns

Capture:

- cycle regularity and symptom timing
- PMS or PMDD features
- pregnancy and postpartum transitions
- menopause or perimenopause shifts
- libido, hair, skin, weight, temperature tolerance
- hormonal contraception or hormone therapy
- endocrine workup and treatment response

What moves the causal map:

- repeatable cycle-linked patterns
- postpartum or menopausal turning points
- endocrine treatments that clearly changed symptoms

## Environment and exposures

Ask when:

- symptoms changed with moves, jobs, buildings, hobbies, travel, or chemical exposure

Capture:

- home and workplace changes
- mold, dampness, smoke, dust, solvents, pesticides
- water damage and remediation history
- pets, bites, outdoor exposure, unusual hobbies
- symptom change during travel or time away

What moves the causal map:

- exposure-linked onset or relief
- location-specific flares
- recurrent inflammatory or respiratory patterns

## Infections and inflammatory episodes

Ask when:

- onset followed an infection, inflammatory event, vaccination, travel illness, or recurrent flare pattern

Capture:

- specific infections and dates
- severity and recovery pattern
- incomplete recovery after infection
- recurrent fevers, swollen nodes, rashes, mouth ulcers
- vaccination timing if clearly relevant

What moves the causal map:

- post-infectious onset
- recurrent inflammatory bursts
- partial but meaningful response to anti-inflammatory approaches

## Early life and upbringing

Ask when:

- developmental, family-system, nutritional, or early-environment context may explain vulnerability

Capture:

- childhood health pattern
- developmental issues if relevant
- household stress or instability
- diet, sleep, exposures, and activity norms
- major childhood illnesses or injuries

What moves the causal map:

- vulnerability factors that shape later responses
- longstanding baseline traits mistaken for recent illness

## Trauma and adversity

Ask only when relevant and with care.

Capture:

- whether major adverse experiences occurred
- whether the user wants to discuss them
- whether they appear to shape physiology, coping, or symptom persistence

What moves the causal map:

- trauma-linked hyperarousal, sleep disruption, pain amplification, GI shifts, or autonomic instability

Do not force detail. Capture only what is necessary to orient the case.

## Prior workup and clinician journey

Ask when:

- the user has already done substantial testing or consulted multiple clinicians

Capture:

- tests, imaging, procedures, and major findings
- normal workup that already retired common explanations
- specialist impressions
- treatments tried and what happened
- points where the medical journey itself became a burden

What moves the causal map:

- repeated negative workup that changes differential emphasis
- treatment failures or paradoxical responses
- evidence already answering questions that would otherwise be re-asked

## Practical constraints

Ask in most substantial cases.

Capture:

- time, energy, money, caregiving, food access, schedule, work constraints
- adherence barriers
- what the user can realistically do next
- urgency of upcoming appointments or deadlines

What moves the causal map:

- constraints that shape interpretation, next steps, and output usefulness
