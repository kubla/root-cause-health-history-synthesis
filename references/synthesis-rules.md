# Synthesis Rules

The goal is not exhaustive transcription. The goal is high-signal compression that preserves timing, structure, uncertainty, and usefulness.

## Compression rules

- keep the timeline legible
- preserve major turning points
- group facts into clusters instead of lists where possible
- prefer what changed, when, and what mattered over encyclopedic detail
- preserve contradictions and missing information explicitly
- keep practical constraints visible enough to affect interpretation

## Writing the one-line summary

A strong one-line summary should include:

- who the case is about at a useful level of abstraction
- the dominant problem or cluster
- the overall time course
- the most important contextual qualifier

Pattern:

`Person with [dominant cluster or problem] developing over [time course] after or alongside [key turning point], now limited by [current bottleneck].`

Good one-line summaries compress the case without pretending certainty.

## Drafting the narrative spine

The narrative spine should answer:

- when the user was last mostly well
- what changed first
- what changed next
- what the major inflection points were
- what the present state looks like
- why the case matters now

If the story cannot yet be told in sequence, the case is not ready for a polished artifact.

## Drafting symptom clusters

Each cluster should capture:

- what belongs in the cluster
- when it started
- how it behaves over time
- what worsens it
- what relieves it
- how it affects function
- which treatments or exposures seem linked

A cluster is useful when it can drive hypotheses, not just describe discomfort.

## Translating case facts into causal factors

Use these factor types:

- antecedent
- trigger
- mediator
- perpetuator
- protective factor

For each factor, state:

- what it is
- why it is in the map
- what period it belongs to
- which clusters it touches
- what evidence supports it
- how confident the case is
- whether it is modifiable

Prefer cautious mechanism-shaped language over vague storytelling.

## Contradictions and missing information

Contradictions should be stated plainly:

- self-report conflicts with imported evidence
- dates are uncertain or inconsistent
- treatments have conflicting effects across time
- multiple mechanisms fit the same pattern

Missing information should be prioritized, not dumped. Focus on what would change the case most.

## Audience transformations

The master case stays canonical.

- Clinician renderings should be concise, skimmable, and timeline-forward.
- AI renderings should be more explicit about provenance, structure, contradictions, and reasoning prompts.

Do not create separate truths for separate audiences.
